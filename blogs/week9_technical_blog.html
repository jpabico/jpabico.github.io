A Beginner's Guide to Regular Expressions

May 8, 2014

A regular expression (or "regex") is a sequence of characters that forms a search pattern. Each character in a regular expression represents a literal meaning or a variety of special meanings. Here, I will show some common regular expressions and their meaning so you can learn by example. All regular expressions are written between two forward slashes / -regular expression goes here- /. A caret ^ indicates the beginning of the expression. A dollar sign $ indicates the end of the expression.

Matching a User Name

/^[a-z0-9_-]{3,16}$/
  • ^ beginning of expression
  • [a-z0-9_-] any lowercase letter (a-z), number (0-9), an underscore, or a hyphen
  • {3,16} at least 3 characters but no more than 16
  • $ end of expression

Matching a Password

/^[a-z0-9_-]{6,18}$/
Same as above, but the number of characters allowed is now between 6 and 18.

Matching a Hex Value

/^#?([a-f0-9]{6}|[a-f0-9]{3})$/
  • ^ beginning of expression
  • #? a number sign is optional because it is followed by a question mark
  • {3,16} at least 3 characters but no more than 16
    • () surrounds additional search patterns grouped together because, in this case, there's an 'or' (see below)
  • [a-f0-9] a lowercase letter (a-f) or a number (0-9)
  • {6} exactly 6 characters long
  • | or (the operator, as in "this or that")
  • [a-f0-9] a lowercase letter (a-f) or a number (0-9)
  • {3} exactly 3 characters long
  • $ end of expression

Matching a Slug

/^[a-z0-9-]+$/
  • ^ beginning of expression
  • [a-z0-9-] any lowercase letter (a-z), number (0-9), or a hyphen
  • + followed by one or more (meaning one or more of the characters defined by [a-z0-9-])
  • $ end of expression

Matching an Email

/^([a-z0-9_\.-]+)@([a-z0-9_\.-]+)\.([a-z\.]{2,6})$/
  • ^ beginning of expression
  • () there are 3 groups of expressions here, grouped together by paranethesis
  • note: here, you will often see \. in the expression, which means literally the period character '.' (the back slash is there to escape the character; otherwise a "." means "any 1 single character")
    1. group in 1st set of ()
      • [a-z0-9_\.-] any lowercase letter (a-z), number (0-9), an underscore, a period, or a hyphen
      • + followed by one or more (meaning one or more of the characters defined by [a-z0-9_\.-])
      • @ literally the "@" character (here, it follows the 1st group and precedes 2nd group)
    2. group in 2nd set of ()
      • [a-z0-9_\.-] any lowercase letter (a-z), number (0-9), an underscore, a period, or a hyphen
      • + followed by one or more (meaning one or more of the characters defined by [a-z0-9_\.-])
      • \. literally the "." character (here, it follows the 2nd group and precedes 3rd group)
    3. group in 3rd set of ()
      • [a-z\.] any lowercase letter (a-z), number (0-9), or a period
      • {2,6} at least 2 characters but no more than 6 (because some emails end with .ny.us or co.uk for example)
  • $ end of expression

Matching a URL

/^(https?:\/\/)?([\da-z\.-]+)\.([a-z\.]{2,6})([\/\w \.-]*)*\/?$/
In many ways, this matches the regular expression for matching an email. Special attention to the following:
  • s? the "s" in "https" is optional due to the question mark
  • \/ the back slash \ is the escape character, the actual character is the forward slash /
  • (...)? https:// as a whole is optional due to the question mark
  • \da-z\.- any digit (\d), any lowercase letter (a-z), or a period (\.)
  • \w is equivalent to [A-Za-z0-9_] (any letter, any digit, or an underscore)
  • * means optional, just like the question mark but matches 0 or more times (? is 0 or 1 time)
Now that you have a general idea of how regular expressions work, here are a couple more useful ones. See if you can figure out the meaning using w3schools.com as a source.

Matching an HTML Tag

/^<([a-z]+)([^<]+)*(?:>(.*)<\/\1>|\s+\/>)$/

Matching an IP Address

/^(?:(?:25[0-5]|2[0-4][0-9]|[01]?[0-9][0-9]?)\.){3}(?:25[0-5]|2[0-4][0-9]|[01]?[0-9][0-9]?)$/
Have fun!
