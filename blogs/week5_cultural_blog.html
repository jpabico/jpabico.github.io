Online Education - The Bad that Comes with the Good

April 08, 2014

Massive Open Online Courses (or MOOCs for short) seem to be the answer to many problems that we face in society. But with serveral students in each online course (often in the tens of thousands), it's like managing a small metropolitan city, and some problems get more and more difficult to solve. Some of the advantages of MOOCs include:

  • Providing an education in the absence of a centralized campus. Some people might not have the possibility to travel to/from a bricks and mortar institution
  • Providing a free educations. Money is not a problem
  • Developing a global community. Students get to meet people from around the world and learn to understand different cultures through collaborating with people from other countries.
  • Having the opportunity to chat with some of the best individuals in various fields. Whether it be award-winning musicians, well-travelled historians, or ground-breaking researchers, reputable professors from some of the top universities often teach MOOCs, attracted by the prospect of sharing their knowledge and findings with thousands of students at a time online rather than just a handful in the classroom.
  • Offering a chance to obtain skills and knowledge to improve career opportunities and job prospects
But despite all these advantages, there is one major disadvantage that puts a dark shadow over these benefits: cheating.

How do do ensure that students are doing their own work? Due to the large number of students, grading becomes an issue. Many professors resort to multiple choice and true/false questions so that grading can be automated and provide timely feedback. This calls into question the quality of work and depth of understanding of the students. Not being able to assess how effective the teaching is makes accreditation for online programs more difficult to obtain.

Cheating can take the form of plagerizing, asking others to do your work, using unapproved resources during exams, and registering for the same online class with two different usernames to gain access to exams just to name a few. Are there any means of preventing cheating? There have been a few plausible proposals for helping alleviate this problem. These include:

  • Taking tests at approved regional testing centers (though this might not be convenient or possible for everyone).
  • Having cameras watch you do your work each you're logged in (insert NSA joke here).
  • Screen Sharing. This allows proctors to see in real-time that you're the person actually doing the work as ait's being done.
  • Supplying photo IDs (though this may raise some identity theft issues)
Some people may doubt the effectiveness of the methods mentioned above, but it's more than is currently done in physical classrooms (and might very well be more effective if instituted in a physical classroom).

What does future hold for MOOCs? Nobody can say for sure. I don't believe that online degrees will hold the weight of a degree obtained through a bricks and mortar institution, at least not anytime soon. As long as the issue of cheating is left unresolved, so will the credibility of an online degree be questioned.
